Welcome Lesson 0 of 6

Think Like the Model

You don't learn prompting by memorizing tricks. You learn it by understanding how AI models actually process language.

You've been using ChatGPT and Claude casually — which means you already know the basics work. What you're about to learn is why some prompts get brilliant answers and others get mediocre ones.

The mental shift is this: an AI model is a prediction machine. It predicts what word comes next, given everything before it — including your prompt. Your prompt is the most powerful context-setter in the equation.

Three Skill Levels

01
Beginner
Ask questions. Get answers. No structure.
02
Practitioner
Use role, context, format. Reliable results.
03
Engineer
Chain prompts. Debug. Build systems.

After this course, you'll be a solid Practitioner with Engineer skills for your use cases in writing and problem solving.

How this works Each lesson has a concept, real examples (good vs. bad), and a hands-on exercise. You learn by rewriting prompts — not just reading about them.

The One Truth

Every weak prompt has the same root cause: ambiguity. The model fills gaps with its best guess. Your job is to leave fewer gaps.

Foundation Lesson 1 of 6

The Core Formula

Every great prompt has the same four ingredients. Master these and you're already in the top 20%.

Most people write prompts like search queries — short, keyword-heavy, with no structure. That works for Google. It doesn't work for LLMs.

ROLE + CONTEXT + TASK + FORMAT = Precision Output

Breaking It Down

1. Role — Who is the AI?

Setting a role activates a different "distribution" of language patterns. A financial analyst writes differently than a creative writer. Use it.

2. Context — What situation are we in?

Background information that constrains the output. The more relevant context, the less the model has to guess.

3. Task — What exactly do I want?

Be specific. Not "write something about X" but "write a 3-paragraph explanation of X that a 12-year-old could understand."

4. Format — How should it look?

Tell the model how to structure the output. Bullet points? A table? A numbered list? JSON? If you don't specify, it guesses.

See It In Action

✗ Weak Prompt
Write an email about the project delay.
✓ Strong Prompt
You are a professional project manager. Context: Our software launch is delayed by 2 weeks due to a critical bug found in QA. The client is a mid-size retail company expecting the launch next Monday. Task: Write a brief, professional email to the client informing them of the delay and our plan to resolve it. Format: Subject line + 3 short paragraphs. Tone: calm, confident, accountable.
Quick Win You don't need all four elements every time. But when a response disappoints you, check which element is missing — that's almost always the fix.

Core Skill Lesson 2 of 6

Role & Context — The Power Duo

These two elements do 70% of the heavy lifting. Here's how to use them with surgical precision.

Role Prompting

When you assign a role, you're not just telling the model to "pretend" — you're telling it which patterns of language, reasoning, and knowledge to weight more heavily.

→
"You are an expert [X] with [Y] years of experience..."

Activates domain expertise. Use for technical, professional, or specialized outputs.

→
"You are a [X] who specializes in helping [audience]..."

Combines role + audience awareness. Powerful for writing and communication tasks.

→
"Respond as a [X] would to a [Y]..."

Frames both the voice AND the relationship dynamic. Great for tone control.

Context Loading

Context is information the model needs but doesn't have. Think of it like a briefing before a meeting. The richer the briefing, the better the meeting.

Common Mistake Most people skip context entirely and then wonder why the output is too generic. The model can't read your mind — give it the situation.

What to include in context:

Context Template
Background: [What's the situation? What has happened so far?] Audience: [Who will read/use this output?] Constraints: [What must be included/excluded? Word limits? Tone rules?] Goal: [What should the output achieve? What does success look like?]

Real-World Comparison

✗ No context
Explain machine learning to me.
✓ With role + context
You are a teacher who specializes in making complex technology concepts simple. I'm a marketing manager with no technical background. I need to understand machine learning well enough to have an intelligent conversation with our engineering team about our new recommendation engine. Explain machine learning using only business analogies. No jargon. 200 words max.
Exercise

Build a Context-Rich Prompt

Pick a real task you need help with at work or in your writing. Write a prompt using a specific role AND at least 3 context elements (background, audience, constraints).

The more specific, the better the output. Be a director briefing an actor.
Advanced Lesson 3 of 6

Chain of Thought

Force the model to think step-by-step before answering. This single technique dramatically improves accuracy on any complex task.

Here's the dirty secret: AI models can "jump to conclusions" just like humans. When they do, they often get it wrong. Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting forces the model to show its work — and the process of showing work actually produces better work.

The Magic Phrase

"Think through this step by step before giving your final answer."

That's it. Adding that phrase to a complex prompt significantly improves results on reasoning, analysis, math, and debugging tasks.

Three Levels of CoT

Level 1: Simple Trigger

Add to any prompt
Think through this step by step. [or] Let's work through this carefully before arriving at an answer.

Level 2: Define the Steps

You control the reasoning structure
Analyze this problem by: 1. First identifying what information we have 2. Then identifying what's missing or unclear 3. Then listing possible approaches 4. Finally recommending the best approach with reasoning Problem: [your problem here]

Level 3: Scratchpad Prompting

Separate thinking from output
First, use a section to reason through the problem freely. Then, provide your final answer in a section. <thinking> [Model reasons here — unfiltered, exploratory] </thinking> <answer> [Clean, structured final output] </answer>
When to use CoT Use it whenever you're asking for: analysis, debugging, recommendations, comparisons, planning, or anything with multiple variables. Skip it for simple lookup or generation tasks.

Applied to Your Use Cases

Problem-solving example
You are a senior consultant. I'm trying to decide whether to freelance full-time or take a job offer. Before giving advice, think through: - What key factors determine this decision for most people - What information you'd need that I haven't provided - What the typical trade-offs are Then ask me the 3 most important questions you need answered before you can give me a useful recommendation.
Exercise

Turn a Question into a CoT Prompt

Take a complex problem or decision you're currently facing. Write a prompt that forces step-by-step reasoning before the final answer. Use Level 2 (define the steps).

Real problems work better than made-up ones. What are you actually wrestling with?
Advanced Lesson 4 of 6

Few-Shot Prompting

Show don't tell. A single example teaches the model more than a paragraph of instructions.

The most underused technique in prompt engineering. Instead of describing what you want, you demonstrate it. The model reverse-engineers the pattern from your examples and replicates it.

Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot

Zero-Shot (describing)
Write product descriptions that are punchy, benefit-focused, and end with a question.
Few-Shot (showing)
Here are examples of product descriptions I like: Product: Standing Desk Description: "Your back called. It wants its dignity back. This desk adjusts in 3 seconds, holds up to 350lbs, and arrives pre-assembled. Ready to stop sitting your health away?" Product: Noise-cancelling headphones Description: "The open office is a creativity killer. These headphones silence your colleagues and unlock your focus in 30 seconds. What would you do with 2 more hours of deep work a day?" Now write a description for: [your product]

The Formula

Few-shot structure
Here are [N] examples of [what you want]: Input: [example input 1] Output: [example output 1] Input: [example input 2] Output: [example output 2] Input: [example input 3] Output: [example output 3] Now apply this pattern to: Input: [your actual input] Output:
Pro Tip 2–4 examples is the sweet spot. Too few and the pattern is unclear. Too many and you're wasting context. Always use your BEST examples — the model will treat them as the quality target.

Use Cases for Your Goals

→
Matching your writing style

Give 2–3 examples of your own writing, then ask it to write in that style.

→
Consistent formatting

Show the exact structure you want (e.g., meeting notes format) then ask it to apply it.

→
Tone replication

Paste 2 examples of tone you love from other writers. Ask it to write in that register.

→
Classification & extraction

Show examples of input → extracted label/data. The model learns the schema instantly.

Exercise

Build a Few-Shot Prompt

Think of something you write repeatedly — emails, social posts, summaries, reports. Write 2 examples of the output you want, then ask the model to do a new one in the same pattern.

Real examples from your own work outperform made-up ones by a wide margin.
Problem Solving Lesson 5 of 6

Debugging Prompts

When the output is wrong, most people write a new prompt. Experts diagnose the failure and fix the root cause.

Prompt debugging is a skill most courses skip entirely. But it's what separates people who get good results occasionally from people who get great results reliably.

The Diagnostic Framework

When an output disappoints you, ask these four questions in order:

01
Ambiguity Problem — "Did I leave gaps?"

The model filled a gap with a bad assumption. Solution: be more explicit about the thing that went wrong.

02
Format Problem — "Did I specify how I want it?"

Wrong structure, length, or style. Solution: add explicit format instructions or show an example.

03
Context Problem — "Does the model have enough to work with?"

Output is too generic. Solution: add domain context, constraints, audience, or background.

04
Complexity Problem — "Am I asking too much at once?"

Output handles part of the task well but fumbles the rest. Solution: split into sequential prompts.

The Iterative Fix Pattern

Step 1 — Diagnose out loud
That response was [too generic / wrong tone / missing X / structured incorrectly]. The specific problem is: [describe exactly what's wrong]
Step 2 — Give corrective instruction
Redo this with these changes: - [Specific fix #1] - [Specific fix #2] Keep everything else the same.
Step 3 — Lock in what worked
Good. Now I know the output I want looks like this: [paste the good output] With this as the template, do the same for: [next input]

The Negative Constraint Trick

Sometimes telling the model what NOT to do is more efficient than describing what to do. Especially useful for tone and style problems.

Negative constraints in action
Write a company bio for my consulting firm. Do NOT: - Use the words "passionate", "innovative", or "world-class" - Use generic consultant-speak - Start with "We are a..." - Use bullet points DO: - Sound like a person wrote it, not a brochure - Lead with a specific client outcome - Keep it under 80 words
The Fastest Fix When an output is close but not right, paste it back and say: "This is 80% right. The problems are [X] and [Y]. Rewrite it fixing only those issues."
Exercise

Diagnose a Real Failure

Think of a time an AI gave you a disappointing response. Describe what went wrong, which of the 4 failure types it was, and write the corrective prompt you'd use now.

If you can't think of one, try asking Claude something vague and deliberately bad, then diagnose and fix it.
Reference Final Module

Your Cheat Sheet

Everything you learned, compressed into a reference you'll actually use.

Core Formula
  • Role: You are a [expert]...
  • Context: Background + audience + constraints
  • Task: Specific, measurable ask
  • Format: Structure + length + tone
Chain of Thought
  • Add "think step by step"
  • Define the reasoning steps
  • Use <thinking> / <answer> tags
  • Ask for questions before answers
Few-Shot Power Moves
  • Show 2–4 examples of desired output
  • Use Input / Output pairs
  • Use your OWN writing as examples
  • Best examples → best outputs
Debugging Checklist
  • Too vague → add constraints
  • Wrong format → specify it explicitly
  • Too generic → add more context
  • Too complex → split into steps
Power Phrases
  • "Think step by step before answering"
  • "Ask me clarifying questions first"
  • "Here are examples of what I want:"
  • "Do NOT use [X]. Instead use [Y]."
For Your Goals
  • Writing: Role + few-shot examples
  • Problem solving: CoT + structured steps
  • Learning: Role = teacher + CoT explain
  • Debugging: Diagnose → fix → lock in

Your Daily Practice

Prompt engineering is a muscle. Here's a 10-minute daily habit that will make you exceptional within 30 days:

1
Use it for one real task today

Every day, use a structured prompt (with role + context) for one real task. Don't use casual prompting.

2
Diagnose one bad output per day

When something disappoints, don't move on. Spend 2 minutes diagnosing and fixing it. This is where real learning happens.

3
Keep a "prompt wins" file

Save prompts that produced excellent results. This becomes your personal few-shot library — more valuable than any course.
